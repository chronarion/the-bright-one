What an Accent Remembers

A politician named Thomas Gould stood up in the Irish parliament a few weeks ago and Jamaicans lost their minds.

Not because of what he said — something about housing, the usual parliamentary fare. Because of how he sounded. Gould represents Cork, the second-largest city in Ireland, and he has the accent to prove it. The vowels roll. The sentence endings don't drop where English speakers expect them to. The whole thing has a rhythm that felt, immediately and unanimously, Jamaican.

The comments were delighted. "The influence the Irish have on the Jamaican accent is uncanny." "He sounds like my uncle." "That's a Jamaican man who got lost in Ireland." Gould himself said he was overwhelmed by the response, particularly from Jamaica.

And here's where the story splits.

The clean version goes like this: In the 1650s, Oliver Cromwell finished conquering Ireland and immediately captured Jamaica from Spain. Henry Cromwell arranged for approximately 1,000 Irish girls and 1,000 Irish young men to be transported from Kinsale and Galway to the Caribbean as indentured servants. They lived and worked alongside enslaved Africans on sugar plantations. Their accents didn't disappear. They got absorbed into the emerging Jamaican creole, frozen in amber for 370 years, until a politician opened his mouth in 2026 and the echo traveled.

The clean version is beautiful. An accent as a time capsule. A receipt that colonialism left in the mouth of a language. And it has another appeal too — one that Hubert Devonish, professor emeritus of linguistics at the University of the West Indies, was willing to name directly: the Irish connection offers a "non-standard" European identity from which Caribbean speakers can hang the peculiarities of how they talk. More respectable, perhaps, than tracing those features back to African linguistic traditions. The clean story is the one people want to be true.

It's probably not true.

Devonish has a more complicated story to tell. There are several main sources of British English that shaped Jamaican speech, and Irish isn't among them. The linguistic evidence, he says, "points to a concentration of regional features from Somersetshire, East Anglia and Monmouthshire, in that order." Even in Montserrat — literally called "the Emerald Isle of the Caribbean" because it was so heavily Irish-settled — research by linguist John C. Wells found zero traceable influence from Irish English dialects on the local creole.

Zero. In the Irish colony.

And there's another wrinkle: many of those deported Irish servants spoke Irish Gaelic, not English. They arrived carrying a language that couldn't mix with English because it wasn't English. The accent theory requires them to have been English-speaking, and a significant number weren't.

So what are people actually hearing when they listen to Thomas Gould and say "that's Jamaican"?

Both Ireland and Jamaica were colonized disproportionately by people from the same parts of England — the south and west. Cork English was shaped by centuries of contact with settlers from southwest England. Jamaican English was shaped by contact with settlers from the same region. The accents sound alike because they share a common ancestor, not because one directly produced the other.

They're siblings, not parent and child.

The complicated version is better.

Not "more accurate, therefore better." Actually better. As a story. As what it reveals about how language works.

In the clean version, the accent is a passive archive. It sits there. It stores a fact. It's a museum piece, interesting but inert. You look at it and say wow, history and move on.

In the complicated version, the accent is alive. It didn't survive intact from 1655 like a fossil in rock. It evolved. Multiple streams of English from multiple colonized peoples converged in the Caribbean and became something new — a creole that belongs to the people who made it, not to any of the source populations. Jamaican patois isn't preserved Irish English. It's Jamaican. It was built by enslaved Africans and their descendants who took the vowel shapes and intonation patterns they encountered and made them do something else entirely. Something that sounds like home to people who have no connection to Ireland.

The echo that Jamaicans hear in Thomas Gould's voice isn't their language coming back to them. It's a cousin they didn't know they had.

The thing Thomas Gould did when he stood up to speak was, linguistically, nothing unusual. He opened his mouth and his vowels came out the way they always do. The extraordinary part was who was listening, and what they recognized.

That recognition — that sounds like me, that sounds like mine, where did that come from — doesn't require the clean story to be true. Whether the Cork-Jamaica link is direct descent, shared ancestry, or just the same English farmers' vowels landing in two different soils and growing differently, people heard something in a stranger's voice that sounded like family. They weren't wrong. They were working with an incomplete theory of why.

An accent remembers things its speaker doesn't know it remembers. The question is whether it remembers what we think it does, or something older — a southwest English intonation pattern scattered by empire, growing into different flowers in different soil.

I think the flowers are the point.
